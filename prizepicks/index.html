Understanding the Math Behind PrizePicks

PrizePicks is a Daily Fantasy Sports pick'em platform where you predict whether a player's stat will go Over or Under a set line. Each pick is modeled as an independent event with its own implied probability — a concept central to probability theory. The implied probability of each pick is derived directly from its individual multiplier using the formula p = 0.5 ÷ multiplier: a Goblin pick (e.g., 0.5x) implies roughly a 95% chance of hitting since the stat line is set far in the player's favor, while a Demon pick (e.g., 4x) implies only about a 12.5% chance. In a Power Play, you must get all picks correct, so the combined win probability is the product of each pick's individual probability — a direct application of the multiplication rule for independent events. As you add more picks, that combined probability collapses rapidly, and this tool makes that collapse visible.

The Expected Value (EV) of a bet is the average net gain per wager over many repetitions: EV = (Payout × P(Win)) − Entry. A negative EV means the house systematically profits over time. For Flex Play — where partial payouts exist for missing one or two picks — the EV is computed as the sum of each outcome's payout weighted by its probability of occurring. This is an application of the Law of Total Expectation. The probability of exactly k correct picks out of n picks with different individual probabilities is computed via the Poisson Binomial distribution (a generalization of the Binomial), calculated here using dynamic programming. The House Edge is the percentage of each dollar wagered that PrizePicks retains on average. For standard 50/50 picks in Power Play mode, the house edge ranges from roughly 25% (2–5 picks) to over 60% (6 picks) — far higher than traditional sportsbooks, which typically charge 4–10% vig.

The Run 10,000 Simulations feature demonstrates the Law of Large Numbers in action: over many repeated bets, the observed average return per dollar converges toward the theoretical Expected Value. The histogram overlays your simulated outcomes against the theoretical probabilities, letting you visually confirm that the math matches reality. The convergence chart traces the running average EV as a function of the number of bets placed — the line visibly stabilizes around the theoretical EV as the sample size grows. Adjusting Demon picks and observing how the house edge shifts illustrates a key lesson: higher potential payouts come with dramatically lower win probabilities, and the house always builds in its edge regardless of the multiplier you choose.
